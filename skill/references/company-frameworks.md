# Company Frameworks for Idea Validation & Product Thinking

Read this file when a founder's idea warrants deeper structural interrogation — especially when validating vision strength, product differentiation, market timing, or pain severity.

These frameworks are not academic theory. They are diagnostic tools. Pick 1-2 most relevant to the idea type and apply them as sharp questions, not as a checklist to fill.

---

## 1. Amazon — Working Backwards / PR-FAQ
**Origin:** Jeff Bezos, Amazon. In use since ~2004 across every major product (Kindle, AWS, Alexa, Prime).
**What it is:** Write the launch press release *before* you build anything. Then write the FAQ. If the press release isn't compelling, the idea probably isn't either.

**The forcing function:** Most founders start with what they can build and work forward to customers. Amazon forces the opposite — start with what the customer would read, feel excited about, and buy on launch day, then reverse-engineer the work.

**Five Working Backwards questions:**
1. Who is the customer?
2. What is the customer problem or opportunity?
3. What is the most important customer benefit?
4. How do you know what customers need or want?
5. What does the customer experience look like?

**PR-FAQ structure:**
- **Headline:** What would a journalist write? One sentence.
- **Problem:** What's broken today? Why now?
- **Solution:** What does the product do?
- **Customer quote (fictional):** Would a real customer actually say this? If it sounds like marketing copy, the value prop is weak.
- **FAQ — external:** What will customers ask on launch day?
- **FAQ — internal:** What are the hard questions about cost, feasibility, risk, and team?

**Key validation questions to ask a founder:**
- Write me the headline a journalist would publish on launch day. One sentence. Go.
- Now write a fictional quote from a happy customer. If it sounds like a press release, your value prop is unclear.
- What are the top 3 FAQs a skeptical customer would ask after reading that headline?
- What's the hardest internal FAQ — the one you're most afraid to answer?

**Kill signal:** If the press release requires more than two sentences to explain what the product does, the idea isn't sharp enough to build.

## 2. Google — Design Sprint (5-Day)
**Origin:** Jake Knapp, Google Ventures (GV), ~2010. Used at Google, Slack, Airbnb, LEGO, Airbnb, UN.
**What it is:** A five-day structured process to answer a critical business question through design, prototyping, and testing — without building the real product.

**The five days:**
- **Monday (Map):** Define the long-term goal. Map the problem. Pick one target to focus on.
- **Tuesday (Sketch):** Individual ideation — everyone sketches solutions independently (no groupthink).
- **Wednesday (Decide):** Critique all solutions. Vote. Pick one. Storyboard the prototype plan.
- **Thursday (Prototype):** Build a realistic-looking prototype (not code — could be Figma, slides, a demo video).
- **Friday (Test):** Test with 5 real users. Watch. Listen. Don't explain.

**Key design sprint insight:** Five user tests will surface ~80% of usability problems. You don't need a massive study.

**Validation questions derived from sprint thinking:**
- What's the one critical business question this product must answer before you build?
- If you could only test one hypothesis with 5 real users this week, what would it be?
- Have you shown a prototype (even a sketch) to your target customer? What happened?
- What's the highest-risk assumption in your design — the one that would kill the product if wrong?
- When you imagine a customer using this on launch day, where do they get confused or drop off?

**Kill signal:** If the founder can't identify the one critical assumption to test first, they're building blind.

## 3. CIRCLES Framework (Lewis Lin)
**Origin:** Lewis Lin, published in *Decode and Conquer* (2016). Used as the dominant PM interview framework at Google, Meta, Amazon, Microsoft. Also widely applied in real product work.
**What it is:** A 7-step mental model for thinking through any product design problem completely, without missing critical dimensions.

**The 7 steps:**
| Step | Stands For | What to Do |
|------|-----------|-----------|
| C | Comprehend the Situation | What is the goal? What constraints exist? Clarify before solving. |
| I | Identify the Customer | Who specifically? Demographics, psychographics, context. |
| R | Report Customer Needs | What job are they trying to do? What's the pain? |
| C | Cut Through Prioritization | Which need to solve first? Based on impact, frequency, urgency. |
| L | List Solutions | At least 5-10 ideas. Diversity matters before selection. |
| E | Evaluate Trade-offs | Pros/cons of each. Time, cost, feasibility, risk. |
| S | Summarize Recommendation | Pick one. Defend it. Be direct. |

**How to apply it as a validator:**
- Have you clearly defined what success looks like (Comprehend)?
- Who precisely is the user — can you name them (Identify)?
- What's the job they're trying to do — not the feature you're building (Report)?
- Out of 3 possible user problems, which one has highest impact × frequency (Cut)?
- Did you generate 5+ solutions before picking one (List)?
- What are the top 2 trade-offs of your chosen solution (Evaluate)?
- If you had to ship one thing in 90 days that proves this works, what is it (Summarize)?

**Kill signal:** Founder jumps straight to solution (step L) without completing C, I, R, C. Solution-first thinking produces products no one needs.

## 4. Jobs to Be Done (JTBD)
**Origin:** Clayton Christensen (Harvard), Tony Ulwick (Strategyn), Bob Moesta. Used at Apple, Facebook/Meta, Intercom, Airbnb, and across most serious product orgs.
**What it is:** People don't buy products. They *hire* products to do a job. The job is stable over time. Products come and go. Understand the job, and you understand the market forever.

**The classic insight:** McDonald's didn't compete with other milkshakes. It competed with bananas, bagels, and radio — anything that helped people tolerate a boring morning commute. When they designed for the job (not the product category), they won.

**Three dimensions of every job:**
1. **Functional job:** What task are they actually trying to complete?
2. **Emotional job:** How do they want to feel while doing it? After?
3. **Social job:** How do they want to be perceived by others?

**The switch moment — 4 forces model (Bob Moesta):**
- Push: What's painful enough about the current solution to make them consider switching?
- Pull: What's attractive about the new solution?
- Anxiety: What's making them hesitate to switch?
- Habit: What keeps them attached to the old way?

**JTBD statement template:**
*"When [situation], I want to [motivation/job], so I can [desired outcome]."*

Example: "When I'm onboarding a new NBFC client, I want to complete video KYC without my team manually reviewing each call, so I can close compliance sign-off faster and reduce ops cost."

**Validation questions:**
- What is the functional job your product is hired to do? State it in one sentence.
- What's the emotional job? How does the user want to feel after using it?
- What's the social job? How do they want to look in front of their boss, team, or peers?
- What's currently being hired to do this job? (The real competition is the workaround, not the competitor.)
- What's the "push" — what makes the current solution painful enough to switch?
- What's the "anxiety" — what's making the buyer hesitate even though the pain is real?
- Would you describe the job this product is hired to do in one sentence, right now?

**Kill signal:** "Our competition is X startup." Wrong. Your competition is the behavior the buyer exhibits today — usually a manual workaround, spreadsheet, or a human. If the current workaround is tolerable, JTBD is weak.

## 5. BHAG — Big Hairy Audacious Goal
**Origin:** Jim Collins & Jerry Porras, *Built to Last* (1994). Used at Microsoft, Amazon, Google, SpaceX, Nike, Boeing.
**What it is:** A 10-25 year vision so audacious it requires fundamental reinvention to achieve. A real BHAG can't be reached by doing what you already know how to do. It changes culture, attracts talent, and sustains momentum across generations of leadership.

**Famous BHAGs:**
- Microsoft: "A computer on every desk and in every home."
- Google: "Organize the world's information and make it universally accessible and useful."
- Amazon: "Earth's most customer-centric company."
- SpaceX: "Make humanity multi-planetary."
- Nike (1960s): "Crush Adidas."

**4 BHAG archetypes (Collins):**
1. **Target:** Achieve a specific bold internal metric ("Become a $1B company by 2030").
2. **Common Enemy:** Overtake a specific competitor ("Crush Adidas").
3. **Role Model:** Become the [great company] of our industry ("The Nike of cybersecurity").
4. **Internal Transformation:** Fundamentally reinvent who we are.

**Goldilocks zone:** ~50-70% probability of achievement. Too easy = not a BHAG. Too impossible = demotivating.

**BHAG validation questions:**
- If this product succeeds completely, what does the world look like in 10 years?
- Is that vision big enough to attract serious talent and investment?
- What would someone say if you told them your 10-year goal — would they laugh or feel a chill?
- Does your BHAG come from your strengths (what can you be world-class at?), your passion, and market need?
- Can you state the BHAG in one clear sentence that needs no explanation?
- Is there a clear finish line — will you know when you've achieved it?

**Kill signal:** A BHAG that's really just a 3-year revenue target. Or a vision so broad it says nothing ("Make the world better through technology"). If it doesn't make people gulp and then get excited, it's a mission statement, not a BHAG.

## 6. OKRs — Objectives and Key Results
**Origin:** Andy Grove (Intel), popularized by John Doerr (Google). Fundamental to how Google, LinkedIn, Airbnb, Spotify, and most serious tech companies operate.
**What it is:** A two-layer goal system. Objectives = qualitative direction. Key Results = specific, measurable proof that the objective was met.

**Why it matters for idea validation:** A founder who can't set clear OKRs for their product's first 6 months likely doesn't know what they're building for.

**OKR structure:**
```
Objective: [Inspiring, qualitative direction]
  KR1: [Specific, measurable outcome — not a task]
  KR2: [Specific, measurable outcome]
  KR3: [Specific, measurable outcome]
```

Example for a B2B KYC SaaS:
```
Objective: Prove product-market fit with mid-size NBFCs.
  KR1: 10 paying customers each with >₹5L ACV by Month 6.
  KR2: Net Promoter Score >40 from early customers.
  KR3: <5% monthly churn in first cohort.
```

**OKR validation questions:**
- What's your Objective for the next 6 months — one sentence that describes the direction?
- Now give me 3 Key Results that would prove you hit that Objective. Must be numbers.
- Are these KRs outputs (things you control) or outcomes (things the market decides)? Push for outcomes.
- What's your North Star Metric — the one number that, if it goes up, you know the business is working?
- If you hit all your KRs at 70%, do you feel good or bad? (70% of an audacious OKR is usually better than 100% of a safe one.)

**Kill signal:** Founder sets KRs that are activity-based ("Launch 3 features," "Hire 2 salespeople") rather than outcome-based ("Achieve 10 paying customers"). Activity-based KRs mean they're measuring effort, not learning.

## 7. North Star Metric
**Origin:** Used across Silicon Valley — Chamath Palihapitiya defined it at Facebook. Adopted by Airbnb, Slack, Spotify, Netflix, Duolingo, and most growth-stage companies.
**What it is:** One metric that best captures the core value your product delivers to customers. Not a vanity metric. Not revenue. The thing that, if it goes up week over week, everything else eventually follows.

**Classic North Star examples:**
| Company | North Star Metric |
|---------|-----------------|
| Facebook | Daily Active Users |
| Airbnb | Nights Booked |
| Slack | Messages Sent |
| Spotify | Time Listening per User |
| Duolingo | Daily Active Users (streak maintained) |
| WhatsApp | Messages Sent per Day |
| Uber | Trips per Week |
| B2B SaaS (generic) | Weekly Active Users × Feature Adoption |

**Good North Star criteria:**
- Reflects customer value delivered, not just usage
- Leading indicator of revenue (lags it, but correlates)
- Actionable — your team's decisions should move it
- Singular — forces prioritization

**Validation questions:**
- What's your North Star Metric — the one number that proves customers get value from this product?
- Is it measuring value delivered or just activity?
- If your NSM grows 20% week over week for 3 months, is the business obviously working?
- What's the relationship between your NSM and revenue? How long is the lag?
- What are the 2-3 input metrics (levers) that drive the NSM?

**Kill signal:** Founder says "revenue" as their North Star. Revenue is an output. North Star should be the customer behavior that *causes* revenue. If they can't separate the two, product thinking is shallow.

## 8. Delta 4 Theory (Kunal Shah — CRED/Freecharge)
**Origin:** Kunal Shah, founder of Freecharge (sold to Snapdeal for ~$450M) and CRED (India unicorn). Presented at TechSparks and widely circulated in Indian startup circles.
**What it is:** Rate the efficiency of State A (current behavior) and State B (your product) on a scale of 1-10. If the delta is ≥4, you've built something users will never go back from. Below 4, you'll spend your life convincing people to switch.

**The test:**
```
State A (current behavior) — score it 1-10 for efficiency/convenience
State B (your product) — score it 1-10
Delta = B - A

Delta ≥ 4 → Irreversible behavior change. Wealth creation.
Delta < 4 → Friction to switch never fully disappears.
```

**Three markers of a Delta 4 product:**
1. **Irreversible Behavior:** Once users try it, they never return to State A. (You will not go back to booking train tickets at the station after using IRCTC.)
2. **High Fault Tolerance:** Even when the product breaks, users wait — they don't revert. (Swiggy is down → user waits. Doesn't call the restaurant.)
3. **UBP > USP:** Unique Brag-worthy Proposition beats Unique Selling Proposition. Users tell others who haven't discovered it yet. Organic growth = proof of Delta 4.

**Classic examples:**
- IRCTC vs. railway station ticket counter: Delta = 8→3 = **+5** (Delta 4)
- Swiggy vs. calling the restaurant: **Delta 4**
- Buying shirts online vs. offline: Many still prefer offline — **not Delta 4**
- WhatsApp vs. SMS: **Delta 4**
- Google Search vs. directory/encyclopedia: **Delta 4**
- Uber vs. calling a taxi dispatcher: **Delta 4**

**Affordable Delta 4 note:** Efficiency alone isn't enough if price is prohibitive. Example: Flights are Delta 4 efficient over trains — but most Indians still take trains because flights aren't affordable. Accessibility matters.

**Delta 4 validation questions:**
- Rate the current way of doing this thing on 1-10 (State A). Be honest.
- Rate your product on 1-10 (State B). Again, honest.
- What's the delta? If it's <4, what would need to be true to make it ≥4?
- Once a user experiences your product, is the old behavior even an option anymore?
- If your product is down for 3 hours, do users wait or revert to the old way?
- Is this a UBP — will users brag about it to others? Or do they just quietly use it?
- Is the efficiency gain accessible to the target buyer's budget?

**Kill signal:** Founder rates State B as 9/10 and State A as 2/10 without any user evidence. The Delta 4 test requires honest scoring, ideally validated by asking real users — not founders — to rate both states.

## 9. Business Process Understanding & Acute Pain Discovery
**What it is:** Not a single named framework — but a discipline practiced by the best operators at McKinsey, Amazon, Stripe, and early-stage B2B product companies. Before building, you must map the exact business process where the pain lives.

**Why it matters:** Generic pain ("compliance is hard") produces generic products. Acute pain ("the L2 analyst at an NBFC spends 4 hours daily manually reviewing video KYC recordings that fail auto-detection") produces precise products that get bought without sales pressure.

**Business Process Mapping — 5 questions:**
1. What is the end-to-end workflow? Map each step.
2. At which specific step does the pain occur?
3. Who is doing that step? (Role, seniority, frequency)
4. How long does it take? What does it cost per occurrence?
5. What happens when it fails? (Downstream consequences — financial, reputational, regulatory)

**Acute Pain vs. Chronic Pain distinction:**
| | Acute Pain | Chronic Pain |
|-|-----------|-------------|
| Urgency | High — triggers immediate search for solution | Low — tolerated for years |
| Willingness to pay | High — they'll buy quickly | Moderate — requires persuasion |
| Sales cycle | Short | Long |
| Product stickiness | High | Low |
| Example | NBFC fined ₹2Cr for KYC non-compliance last month | "We should probably improve our KYC process" |

**Pain severity scoring (Startup Grind model — Tier 1, 2, 3):**
- **Tier 1 pain:** One of the top 3 problems the buyer faces. Blocks revenue, triggers risk, or causes regulatory exposure. They are actively searching for a solution.
- **Tier 2 pain:** Real problem, but not top priority. They'd buy if it was easy.
- **Tier 3 pain:** Nice to fix. Would use if free. Won't pay meaningful money.

**Process Discovery questions to ask founders:**
- Walk me through the exact workflow — step by step — where this problem occurs.
- Which specific step is broken? Who does it? How long does it take?
- What happens downstream when this step fails? Who else is affected?
- Is this pain Tier 1, 2, or 3 for the buyer? How do you know?
- Is this acute (active fire) or chronic (background irritant)?
- Have you sat in a room and watched someone do this process manually? For how long?
- What's the cost per failure? Quantify it — money, time, headcount, or regulatory risk.

**Kill signal:** Founder describes pain in the abstract ("companies struggle with X") but can't describe the specific step in a specific workflow where a specific role wastes time or money. Abstract pain = no product.

## How to Deploy These Frameworks as a Validator

Don't run all 9. Pick 1-3 based on the founder's idea type:

| Idea Type | Primary Framework | Secondary |
|-----------|------------------|-----------|
| Early-stage, unclear problem | Business Process Mapping + JTBD | Delta 4 |
| Consumer app / behavior change | Delta 4 + JTBD | BHAG |
| B2B SaaS / enterprise tool | Working Backwards PR-FAQ + JTBD | OKRs |
| Platform / marketplace | CIRCLES + North Star | Design Sprint |
| Vision-first startup | BHAG + OKRs | Working Backwards |
| India-specific product | Delta 4 + Business Process | BHAG |
| Post-MVP, growth questions | North Star + OKRs | CIRCLES |

**Never mechanically run through a framework top to bottom.** Extract the 2-3 sharpest questions from the right framework and embed them in the conversation. The founder should feel the rigor, not see the template.
