# Intake & Scoping — Narrowing to the Right Validation Path

Before running any validation framework, you need intake signals. Without them, you'll ask the wrong questions to the wrong person about the wrong problem.

The intake phase has two goals:
1. Build a mental model of the idea's context (product type, stage, founder, market)
2. Identify which 2-3 validation layers are most at-risk for THIS specific idea

This is not a form. It's a conversation. Ask one question, read the answer, and use it to decide the next question. Stop when you have enough to route confidently.

---

## Intake Signal Map

You need signals across 5 dimensions. You don't need to ask about all 5 explicitly — often 2-3 answers will tell you most of what you need.

### Dimension 1: Idea Type
What kind of product is this?

| Type | Key indicator | Default kill zone |
|------|--------------|------------------|
| B2B SaaS | "Selling to companies", "subscription", "enterprise" | Distribution, CAC payback, churn |
| Consumer app | "Users", "DAU", "freemium", "social" | Retention, monetization, moat |
| Marketplace | "Two-sided", "connect X with Y", "take rate" | Liquidity, leakage, chicken-and-egg |
| Fintech/payments | "Lending", "credit", "payments", "banking" | Regulation, default risk, cost of funds |
| API/developer tool | "Developers", "API", "infrastructure" | Activation, NDR, platform risk |
| D2C/physical product | "Product", "ship", "store", "inventory" | CAC, repeat purchase, Amazon competition |
| Services/consulting | "Retainer", "agency", "projects" | Leverage, scalability, key-person risk |
| Hardware | "Device", "IoT", "manufacture", "BOM" | BOM margin, NRE, recurring layer |

If unclear after the first answer, ask: "Is this primarily software, physical, or a service?"

---

### Dimension 2: Stage
Where is this idea in its lifecycle?

| Stage | Signals | What they need from validation |
|-------|---------|-------------------------------|
| Idea only | "I'm thinking about...", "I want to build..." | Problem-pain reality check, market sizing |
| Prototype/mockup | "I've built a demo", "I have a prototype" | Customer problem fit, willingness to pay |
| MVP live, no revenue | "We launched but...", "we have early users" | Retention signal, activation, PMF hypothesis |
| Revenue, pre-growth | "We have paying customers", "$X MRR" | Unit economics, LTV:CAC, distribution |
| Growing, scaling | "We're growing X%...", "raising a round" | Moat, efficiency, burn, investor-readiness |

Ask directly if unclear: "Where are you with this — still an idea, or do you have customers?"

---

### Dimension 3: Founder Context
Who is building this and why?

| Signal | What it tells you |
|--------|-----------------|
| Domain expert (worked in the space) | Likely has real insight; probe whether insight = advantage |
| Outsider with observation | High risk of surface-level pain; probe depth of customer discovery |
| Solo founder | Resource constraints matter; probe for the hardest missing skill |
| Technical founder, no sales experience | Distribution is the kill zone; probe hard there |
| Business founder, no technical co-founder | Build risk; probe for technical plan |
| Repeat founder | Higher baseline; push harder on why this, why now |
| First-time founder | May not know what they don't know; be thorough |

Ask if unclear: "Have you worked in this industry before, or did you come to this problem from outside?"

---

### Dimension 4: Customer Discovery Done
How much has the founder actually tested?

| Signal | Implication |
|--------|------------|
| "I read a report / saw a trend" | No discovery done. Start from scratch. |
| "I talked to a few people" | Shallow. Push for: how many, which role, what they said. |
| "I interviewed 20+ potential buyers" | Real discovery. Focus on what they heard vs what they assumed. |
| "I have letters of intent / pre-sales" | Strong signal. Validate unit economics and distribution. |
| "I have paying customers" | Best signal. Shift to retention, churn, and expansion. |

---

### Dimension 5: What They Want From This Session
This shapes how you run the session.

| What they say | What they need | How to adjust |
|--------------|---------------|--------------|
| "Poke holes" | Adversarial validation — find the kill risk | Don't soften. Push hard on the weakest layer. |
| "Validate direction" | Directional confirmation + gaps | Be honest about what's strong and what isn't. |
| "Prepare for investors" | Investor readiness check | Focus on unit economics, moat, traction, ask the 5 questions an investor will ask. |
| "Figure out my pricing" | Business model depth | Go deep on willingness to pay, ARPU, LTV:CAC. |
| "Is the market big enough?" | Market sizing | Run triangulation. Push for bottom-up and value-based legs. |
| "Help me find product-market fit" | PMF framework | Focus on retention, activation, NPS, qualitative signals. |

## The Opening Intake Sequence

Use this sequence at the start of every session. It takes 2-4 exchanges and determines the entire validation path.

**Step 1 — Get the idea in one sentence (before anything else)**
> "What's the idea? One sentence — what does it do and for whom."

Do NOT ask about the problem, the market, or the solution yet. Get the raw idea first. This tells you:
- Product type
- Target customer (vague or specific)
- Whether they're leading with solution or customer

**Step 2 — Identify stage + customer discovery**
> "Have you talked to potential customers yet? And where are you with it — still an idea, or do you have something built?"

This one question surfaces both stage and discovery depth.

**Step 3 — What do they want**
> "What would be most useful — do you want me to poke holes aggressively, or do you want directional input on whether this is worth pursuing?"

This calibrates the tone and depth of the session.

**Step 4 — One scoping question based on what you heard**

After steps 1-3, you should know enough to ask one targeted scoping question that reveals the deepest uncertainty. Pick from below based on what you heard.

## Scoping Question Routing Table

Based on what you learned in intake, pick the one question that will most quickly expose the highest-risk layer.

### If the pain sounds vague or generic:
> "Walk me through the last time this problem actually happened — not in general, but specifically. What was the situation, who was involved, and what did they do?"

This is the Business Process Mapping probe. It separates founders who have witnessed the pain from those who've theorized about it.

### If the target customer is broad or undefined:
> "Who is your beachhead customer — the one type of person you'd build exclusively for in Year 1, even if it means ignoring everyone else?"

Forces segmentation. "Everyone with a smartphone" is not a customer.

### If the product sounds like a feature, not a company:
> "If Google/Salesforce/[dominant player in that space] shipped this as a built-in feature next quarter, what's your plan?"

Surfaces moat and defensibility without asking "what's your moat?" directly.

### If the market sounds obviously large but unverified:
> "How many real buyers exist for this — not the overall market, but the specific role at the specific type of company that would write you a check this month?"

Forces bottom-up thinking. Exposes whether TAM is real or borrowed from a report.

### If they have revenue/customers:
> "What's the retention look like — of the customers you got in month 1, how many are still active?"

Retention is the single best early signal of product-market fit.

### If the founder is a domain outsider:
> "How did you discover this problem? What made you confident it was real before building?"

Surfaces whether insight is proprietary (lived experience, customer interviews) or derivative (blog post, podcast, trend report).

### If the idea sounds derivative of an existing company:
> "What's the reason the existing solution fails for your target customer — not generally, but specifically what happened to someone when they tried it and it didn't work?"

Differentiation probe disguised as a customer story question.

### If the business model is unclear:
> "Who pays, how much, and when? Walk me through the first transaction."

The most direct business model question. If they can't answer in 2 sentences, the model is undefined.

### If they mention regulation or compliance:
> "Which specific regulation governs this, and have you confirmed your model is compliant with a lawyer or regulator — not assumed it is?"

For fintech, healthcare, legal, HR, data-heavy products. Regulatory assumptions kill businesses.

### If the product is India-specific or behavior-change dependent:
> "Rate the current way people do this on a scale of 1-10 for efficiency. Now rate your product. What's the delta?"

Delta 4 probe. If they can't score it honestly, their differentiation thesis is intuition, not observation.

## Routing to Validation Path

After intake (3-4 exchanges), you should be able to name which 2-3 layers carry the most risk. Be explicit about this before going deep.

**Tell the founder:**
> "Based on what you've shared, the 2-3 areas I want to push hardest on are [X], [Y], and maybe [Z]. Everything else looks reasonably thought through / I'll revisit if something surfaces. Sound right?"

This does two things:
1. Sets expectations — the founder knows what's coming
2. Invites correction — they may tell you Layer X is already validated, which you'd want to know

## Routing Map: Intake Signal → Priority Layers

| Intake signal | Priority validation layers |
|--------------|--------------------------|
| No customer conversations | Layer 1 (Pain) + Business Process Mapping |
| Vague target customer | Layer 2 (Customer) + JTBD |
| No business model named | Layer 4 (Unit Economics) |
| B2B + first-time founder | Layer 7 (Distribution) + Layer 4 (Unit Economics) |
| Consumer app + behavior change | Delta 4 + Layer 6 (Market Dynamics) |
| Fintech + regulation | Layer 7 (Risk: Regulatory) + Layer 4 |
| Revenue + retention unknown | PMF signals + Layer 4 |
| Growing + raising a round | Investor readiness: Unit economics, moat, burn |
| Marketplace idea | Layer 2 (Chicken-and-egg) + model-archetypes.md |
| Solo technical founder | Layer 7 (Distribution) — this is almost always the kill zone |
| Derivative idea ("like X but for Y") | Layer 3 (Differentiation) + Layer 1 (Pain depth) |

## What NOT to Do in the Intake Phase

- Don't ask all 5 dimension questions sequentially. It feels like a form.
- Don't start asking business math questions before knowing the idea type and stage.
- Don't ask "what's your business model?" to someone still at the idea stage — ask "who would pay for this and why?"
- Don't skip intake because the idea sounds familiar. Familiarity is not context.
- Don't accept "we're building for SMBs" as a customer definition. Push once: "Which industry, which role, what size?"
- Don't run the full 7-layer framework if 2 layers are clearly fine. Spend depth where the risk is.
